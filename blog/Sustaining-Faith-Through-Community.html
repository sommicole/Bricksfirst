Sustaining Faith Through Community

June 14, 2025

In an age of hyper-individualism, it’s easy to believe that faith is a private journey. But Scripture, church history, and modern discipleship all speak in unison: faith is sustained not in isolation, but in community. We are saved into a body, not just into a belief system.

At Bricks First Publishing, we affirm the personal nature of faith—but we reject the myth of solo Christianity. A faith that flourishes is a faith that’s rooted in Christian fellowship.

Biblical Foundations: We Before Me

The New Testament is unflinching in its communal vision. Paul writes that we are “members of one body” (Romans 12:5), with each part indispensable to the whole. Hebrews 10:24–25 urges believers not to neglect gathering together, because it is in community that we stir one another toward love and good deeds.

The early church didn’t just meet weekly—they shared life. Acts 2:42–47 describes a community marked by fellowship, generosity, prayer, and shared meals. This wasn’t an extracurricular activity. It was the environment where faith took root and grew strong.

Bonhoeffer: Community as Grace

In Life Together, Dietrich Bonhoeffer calls Christian community a “divine reality” and “grace of God.” He writes:

“The physical presence of other Christians is a source of incomparable joy and strength to the believer.”

Bonhoeffer, writing from the context of Nazi resistance and underground seminary life, saw community not as optional, but essential. It was a means of both formation and endurance.

Eugene Peterson: Spiritual Formation in Context

In Christ Plays in Ten Thousand Places, Eugene Peterson wrote that spiritual maturity is not forged in isolation but “worked out in the company of friends.” He called the church the “practice field” of holiness—where grace is not only preached but practiced.

Dallas Willard: From Belief to Transformation

In Renovation of the Heart, Dallas Willard warned against the individualistic lens of modern spirituality:

“Spiritual formation for the Christian is a process of progressively being formed in the image of Christ... but this process is profoundly social.”

Without community, spiritual habits stagnate or drift into self-justification. With it, we are sharpened, held accountable, and encouraged to persevere.

Modern Loneliness and the Church’s Counterculture

Today’s epidemic of loneliness has reached what the U.S. Surgeon General calls “crisis levels.” In this context, the local church becomes more than a building—it becomes a lifeline.

Christian community answers the ache of isolation not just with friendship, but with purpose. It offers a place to be known fully and loved deeply. A place where faith can be both confessed and carried.

Spiritual Practices for Communal Faith

  • Commit to a local church – Show up consistently—not just to attend, but to belong.
  • Join a small group – Real transformation happens in circles, not rows.
  • Practice confession – James 5:16 calls us to confess to one another so we may be healed.
  • Serve together – Shared mission deepens shared faith.
  • Pray with others – Where two or more gather, Christ is present (Matthew 18:20).

Final Word: The Brick of Brotherhood

At Bricks First, we talk a lot about building lives that last. But no one builds alone. You need people—faithful, flawed, grace-filled people—to sustain your faith through the storms.

Christian community isn’t always clean or convenient. But it is sacred. It is where we learn to forgive, to trust, to carry one another’s burdens—and to grow.

If you want a faith that endures, don't go further alone. Go deeper with others.
