Faith and Fantasy

June 14, 2025

How Imagination Opens the Door to Belief

For centuries, fantasy has been a gateway—not just to other worlds, but to deeper truths. Beyond dragons and quests, magic and myth, great fantasy invites us to wrestle with the eternal. It awakens longing. It whispers of a home we’ve never seen. And for many, it becomes the unexpected road that leads to faith.

At Bricks First Publishing, we believe fantasy isn’t just a genre—it’s a tool. A means of communicating truth to hearts that might not hear it otherwise.

Fantasy Isn’t an Escape—It’s an Entry

Some dismiss fantasy as mere escapism. But the best fantasy doesn’t disconnect us from reality; it reorients us to it. It reframes the battle between good and evil, shows us the cost of courage, and makes abstract spiritual ideas tangible.

C.S. Lewis once said that reason is the natural organ of truth, but imagination is the organ of meaning. Through fantasy, we don’t just understand sacrifice or redemption—we feel it.

And that’s the key. Imagination bypasses our defenses. It opens us up. And in that space, seeds of faith can be planted.

The Legacy of C.S. Lewis

You can’t talk about faith and fantasy without starting with Clive Staples Lewis. An Oxford professor and former atheist, Lewis found faith partly through the myths and stories he loved as a child—and then passed that gift on to millions through The Chronicles of Narnia.

Narnia wasn’t just a children’s tale. It was a layered, symbolic world where Aslan’s death and resurrection reflected the gospel with haunting beauty. Countless readers met Jesus through Aslan before they ever opened a Bible.

Lewis proved that fantasy could carry theological weight without preaching—and that truth, when wrapped in wonder, becomes unforgettable.

J.R.R. Tolkien: Myth as Truth

Lewis’s closest friend and creative sparring partner, J.R.R. Tolkien, approached fantasy as a form of sub-creation—humans imitating their Creator by crafting worlds of their own.

Tolkien’s The Lord of the Rings is not an allegory, but it is deeply Christian. Its themes of hope, providence, humility, and the triumph of light over darkness echo the story of Scripture.

For Tolkien, fantasy wasn’t fiction—it was a way of getting to the truth through myth. He believed that the story of Jesus was the one true myth—the fairy tale that actually happened.

Madeleine L’Engle and the Wonder of the Unknown

In A Wrinkle in Time, Madeleine L’Engle blended science fiction, fantasy, and theology into a wild, imaginative tale. She was unafraid to explore spiritual questions in strange and creative ways.

Her books asked deep questions: What is love? What is evil? What does it mean to trust in something you can’t see? For young readers, L’Engle opened the door to a faith that could coexist with curiosity, mystery, and imagination.

Andrew Peterson and the Return of the Sacred Tale

More recently, singer-songwriter turned novelist Andrew Peterson has rekindled the faith-fantasy connection for a new generation. His Wingfeather Saga is rich with redemption, familial love, and spiritual echoes—without ever being heavy-handed.

Peterson reminds us that stories don’t have to wear a cross on the cover to carry the power of one. The sacred can speak through shadow and song, through sorrow and hope.

Why Faith Needs Fantasy

In an age obsessed with facts, fantasy dares to tell the truth sideways. It doesn’t just preach—it paints. It doesn’t demand—it invites. It speaks to the soul in symbols, stories, and songs.

For many people, fantasy was the first place they encountered the idea of a hero who sacrifices himself for others. A kingdom worth dying for. A darkness that couldn’t be overcome by power, only by love.

Fantasy teaches us to hope. To fight. To believe in things we can’t see.

At Bricks First Publishing, we tell stories that matter.
Some are gritty. Some are whimsical. Some walk straight into mystery. But all of them carry bricks—truths you can build a life on.

We believe faith and fantasy aren’t opposites. They’re allies. One sparks belief. The other makes it burn brighter.
