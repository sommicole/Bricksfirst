Building Faith Through Solitude

June 14, 2025

How Sacred Stillness Strengthens the Soul

In a world that celebrates the loud, the fast, and the seen, solitude is subversive. It is also essential. For the Christian, solitude is not a withdrawal from purpose—it is preparation for it. In solitude, we are not escaping the world; we are being re-formed by God so we can be sent back into it.

At Bricks First Publishing, we believe faith is not merely built through information, but through formation—and solitude is one of the foundational bricks.

The Discipline of Solitude: A Lost Practice

In his seminal work, Spiritual Disciplines for the Christian Life, Donald S. Whitney identifies solitude as a non-negotiable discipline for spiritual growth. “The busier we are,” he writes, “the more we need to make time alone with God.” The paradox is striking—those who lead, serve, and create must retreat to renew.

Dallas Willard, in The Spirit of the Disciplines, frames solitude not as withdrawal but as resistance. He writes:

“Solitude well practiced will break the power of busyness, haste, and loneliness. You will see that the world is not on your shoulders after all.”

That truth cuts through the modern Christian life like a double-edged sword. If we’re always on, always reacting, always producing—our faith becomes shallow, reactive, performative. Solitude, then, becomes the crucible where authentic faith is refined.

Jesus and the Quiet Place

Jesus Himself modeled this. The Gospels frequently note that He “withdrew to lonely places and prayed” (Luke 5:16). Before selecting the twelve disciples, Jesus spent the entire night alone with the Father (Luke 6:12). His public ministry was sustained by private intimacy.

John Mark Comer, in The Ruthless Elimination of Hurry, warns that hurry is incompatible with a spiritual life. “If the devil can’t make you sin,” he writes, “he’ll make you busy.” Solitude isn’t a luxury for the contemplative few—it’s a survival strategy for the spiritually serious.

“The noise of the modern world makes us deaf to the voice of God, drowning out the one input we most need.”

If we are not intentional, solitude will be crowded out by screens, schedules, and social pressures. We must fight to protect it.

Bonhoeffer’s Warning

Dietrich Bonhoeffer, in Life Together, writes with prophetic clarity:

“Let him who cannot be alone beware of community. Let him who is not in community beware of being alone.”

Here, Bonhoeffer highlights the rhythm necessary for a mature faith: we must alternate between solitude and community. Too much solitude, and we risk isolation. Too little, and we risk shallowness.

Solitude gives community depth. Without it, we speak before we listen, act before we pray, lead before we’ve been led.

Solitude Is Not Emptiness—It’s Presence

Solitude is not about emptying the mind, but being filled with the presence of God. It’s not escape—it’s engagement with God on His terms, in His timing, in His silence. It’s where we remember we’re not God and where we learn again to trust Him as God.

If spiritual disciplines are habits of grace, then solitude is the gateway. Without it, we perform. With it, we become.

Practicing Solitude

If you’re ready to build faith through solitude, start small:

  • Begin with 10 minutes of silence each morning, before screens, to simply be with God.
  • Take one day each month for extended solitude: leave your phone, bring your Bible and journal.
  • Build tech-free zones in your home or Sabbath rhythm.
  • Memorize Psalm 46:10: “Be still and know that I am God.”

Final Thought

Solitude is not a suggestion; it is a spiritual necessity. It is how leaders stay grounded, how creators hear clearly, how believers are formed deeply. In a noisy world, silence is faith. In a hurried world, stillness is strength.

If you want to build a life of faith that lasts—start by learning to be alone with God.
